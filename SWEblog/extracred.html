The Ethical Software Engineer: The Suggestion Box


Given this conflict, I would rather not determine the identity of the alleged saboteur.

When a company makes a promise to its employees or its customers, it should do everything in its power to keep that promise. This is not only a matter of ethics, but also culture. When a promise is broken towards employees, there would be a decrease in trust towards management and the company culture would change dramatically. In the case of The Suggestion Box problem, the president is effectively saying that he places the performance of his company over the well-being of his employees. I do not find that effective for solving problems nor productive for the health of the company. An anonymous feedback program is in place so employees can voice their concerns without fear of retaliation. This allows honest feedback to occur and gives management a relatively accurate understanding of how employees feel about their job. If this privacy were to be taken away without notice, employees would no longer provide honest feedback. This lack of trust could also carry over to other aspects of the company. Employees may no longer believe that their future in the company is certain. They might become wary of the president's other policies. If I were to identify the alleged saboteur, all of these things would most likely occur. Instead, it would be more professional and ethical for the president to address the issue publicly, towards all the company's employees. This way, he can prove that he cares about the performance of the company while maintaining the relationships and trust of his employees.

The saboteur's actions are most likely unethical and may cost the company quite a bit of resources. However, this does not mean that the company also has to retaliate in an unethical manner. When a company proves itself to be trustworthy, it will benefit in the long run, as the employees who comprise a company are just as important as the company itself. Additionally, it is the company's responsibility to review each employee's work. An employee should not have the ability to manufacture a product that is defective at such a high frequency. The resources and capital that the company loses from the sabotage can be replaced, but broken trust is much more difficult to repair.

That being said, in some circumstances, it would be best for the company to identify the employee. Let's assume that the employee works at a car manufacturer, and retaliates by removing the driver-side airbag in every ten production vehicles. Over the course of a year, the employee will have removed airbags from thousands of vehicles. Now, consumers are driving vehicles that are not equipped with enough safety equipment. Let's say that people who purchase cars from this manufacturer begin to report injuries from car accidents. They say that their airbag did not deploy at all. Over time, deaths may even arise from this issue. Now, the company has no choice but to identify the person who is sabotaging its vehicles. In this case, employees would most likely understand that the situation is dire. There is no excuse for loss of human life when it can be prevented.
